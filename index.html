Exploring the Seven Wonders of the World 💗

Blog

The 7 Wonders of the World were chosen through a global poll and announced in 2007. Here they are:

  1. Great Wall of China

    2. The Great Wall of China is an ancient series of walls and fortifications, spanning over 13,000 miles (21,000 kilometers) across northern China. Construction began as early as the 7th century BC, with most of the existing wall built during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644). The wall was initially constructed to protect Chinese states from invasions by nomadic groups from the north, like the Mongols and Xiongnu, as well as to control immigration and emigration along the Silk Road. The wall was built with various materials depending on the region, such as tamped earth, wood, bricks, and stones, showcasing impressive ancient engineering skills. Important parts of the structure include watchtowers, garrison stations, and signal towers for communication purposes. The Badaling section, near Beijing, is the most well-preserved and popular among tourists. Symbolically, the Great Wall represents China's enduring strength and unity. In 1987, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and in 2007, it was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World through a global poll. Today, it remains one of the most famous landmarks in the world and an enduring symbol of Chinese culture and history.see more

  2. Chichén Itzá

    3. Chichén Itzá is an ancient Mayan city located in the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico, renowned for its rich history, impressive architecture, and astronomical significance. It was a major center of the Mayan civilization from around 600 to 1200 AD and remained a site of pilgrimage and reverence until the arrival of the Spanish. The site features a variety of architectural marvels, with El Castillo (Temple of Kukulcán) being the most iconic. This pyramid stands as a testament to the Mayans’ advanced understanding of astronomy: during the equinoxes, the play of light and shadow creates the appearance of a serpent slithering down the pyramid's side, symbolizing the god Kukulcán. Other notable structures include the Great Ball Court, used for the Mesoamerican ballgame, and the Temple of the Warriors. Chichén Itzá combines elements of Maya, Toltec, and other Mesoamerican cultures, and as such, represents a fusion of styles and traditions. It was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1988 and is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, a title awarded in 2007 through a global poll. Today, it is one of Mexico's most visited archaeological sites, attracting millions of visitors annually to explore its historical and cultural significance.see more

  3. Petra

    4. Petra is an ancient city carved into pink sandstone cliffs in the southern Jordanian desert, famously known as the “Rose City.” Built around the 4th century BC by the Nabateans, an Arab civilization, it was a major trading hub connecting Asia, Africa, and Europe, where caravans carried spices, silks, and other goods. Petra's strategic location along key trade routes allowed the Nabateans to prosper and exert influence over the region. Petra’s architecture combines Hellenistic, Roman, and local Nabatean styles, with impressive structures carved directly into the sandstone rock. Key monuments include:

    • Al-Khazneh (The Treasury): Petra’s most iconic structure, believed to have been a royal tomb or temple, featuring intricate Corinthian columns and decorative carvings.
    • The Monastery (Ad Deir): One of Petra's largest structures, similar in design to the Treasury but located higher up and offering sweeping views of the area.
    • The Siq: A narrow, winding gorge that serves as the entrance to the city, flanked by high cliffs.
    Petra was largely forgotten in the modern era until Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt rediscovered it in 1812. Today, Petra is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007. Its unique blend of natural beauty and ancient architecture draws visitors worldwide and is a symbol of Jordan's rich cultural heritage.see more

  4. Machu Picchu

    5. Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Incan citadel located in the Andes Mountains of southern Peru, perched at 2,430 meters (7,970 feet) above sea level. Built under the rule of the Incan emperor Pachacuti around 1450, Machu Picchu served as a royal estate and religious site, blending Incan architectural expertise with natural surroundings. Its most famous features include:

    • Intihuatana: A stone structure thought to be an astronomical clock or calendar, used to observe the sun’s position.
    • Temple of the Sun: A semicircular temple used in ceremonies dedicated to the Incan sun god.
    • Temple of the Three Windows: Positioned to capture sunrise views, symbolizing creation myths and the Incan connection to the cosmos.
    The site was constructed in a classical Incan style, with precisely cut dry-stone walls, an impressive technique that has withstood centuries of earthquakes. The city's strategic location and elevated vantage provided defense and control over surrounding valleys. After the fall of the Inca Empire to the Spanish in the 16th century, Machu Picchu was largely abandoned and forgotten by the Western world until American explorer Hiram Bingham brought it to international attention in 1911. Declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007, Machu Picchu remains one of the most visited and significant archaeological sites in the world. Recent studies suggest that the Incas originally named the site "Huayna Picchu," after a nearby peak. Today, Machu Picchu is a symbol of Incan ingenuity and resilience and a testament to their reverence for nature and spirituality. see more

  5. Christ the Redeemer

    6. Christ the Redeemer is a monumental statue of Jesus Christ located atop Mount Corcovado in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Standing 30 meters (98 feet) tall with an arm span of 28 meters (92 feet), the statue was completed in 1931 and has since become one of the most recognizable landmarks in the world. Designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa, sculpted by French artist Paul Landowski, and constructed by the Brazilian engineer Albert Caquot, Christ the Redeemer is made of reinforced concrete and covered with soapstone tiles, chosen for their durability and smooth texture. Its Art Deco style represents both religious devotion and Brazilian artistry, symbolizing peace, openness, and the protection of Rio's residents. The statue’s location on Corcovado Mountain provides a panoramic view of Rio de Janeiro, including sights like Sugarloaf Mountain, the beaches of Copacabana, and the sprawling city below. To access it, visitors can either hike or take a train up the mountain, passing through the lush Tijuca Forest, one of the world’s largest urban forests. In 2007, Christ the Redeemer was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, solidifying its status as a cultural and religious icon. It attracts nearly two million visitors annually and has become a powerful symbol of both faith and national pride in Brazil.see more

  6. Colosseum

    7. The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is an ancient Roman amphitheater located in the heart of Rome, Italy. Commissioned by Emperor Vespasian of the Flavian dynasty in AD 70–72 and completed under his son, Emperor Titus, in AD 80, the Colosseum was designed to host large public spectacles, gladiator combats, animal hunts, and dramas based on Classical mythology. At its peak, it could hold between 50,000 and 80,000 spectators. Constructed primarily of limestone, volcanic rock, and concrete, the Colosseum was a marvel of Roman engineering. Its unique elliptical shape and tiered seating enabled efficient crowd management, and its complex system of vaults, passages, and stairways allowed for smooth movement throughout the arena. Below the main floor, the hypogeum (an underground network of tunnels and cages) housed gladiators and animals, which were raised into the arena through trapdoors for dramatic entrances. Over centuries, the Colosseum suffered damage from earthquakes, stone robbers, and neglect, yet it remains an iconic symbol of ancient Rome’s architectural and cultural achievements. In 1980, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and in 2007, it was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. Today, it attracts millions of tourists annually, symbolizing both the grandeur and complexities of Roman civilization.see more

  7. Taj Mahal

    8. The Taj Mahal is an ivory-white marble mausoleum located in Agra, India, built by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan between 1632 and 1648 in memory of his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal. Known for its symmetrical beauty and intricate craftsmanship, the Taj Mahal is a masterpiece of Mughal architecture, combining elements from Islamic, Persian, Ottoman, and Indian architectural styles. The mausoleum is set on a vast 17-hectare complex with lush gardens, reflecting pools, and red sandstone structures, including a mosque and a guest house. The central structure features a large dome, often described as an "onion dome," flanked by four smaller domes and surrounded by minarets. The marble is adorned with inlaid precious and semi-precious stones in floral and geometric patterns, a technique known as pietra dura. The Taj Mahal’s color changes subtly throughout the day, reflecting different hues at sunrise, noon, and sunset, symbolizing the ever-changing moods of love. It is also noted for its symmetrical layout, including a precisely balanced garden divided into quadrants, symbolizing the paradise described in the Quran. Designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983, the Taj Mahal is widely recognized as an architectural wonder and a symbol of love. In 2007, it was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, drawing millions of visitors annually.see more
